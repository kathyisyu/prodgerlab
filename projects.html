Our Work

Approximately 38 million people worldwide are currently infected with Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV). Two thirds of people living with HIV (PLWH) reside in sub-Saharan Africa, where 90% of infections are from heterosexual sex. The Prodger lab’s research can be divided into two different yet complementary streams

Novel Approaches to Prevent Penile HIV Acquisition

Circumcision reduces a man’s changes of getting HIV through heterosexual sex by at least 60%, but we still don’t fully understand why removing the foreskin is protective. Our team (including collaborators at George Washington University, Johns Hopkins, and the University of Toronto) have determined that at least part of the protective effect of circumcision is conferred by altering the local penile “microbiome”. A microbiome is a diverse polymicrobial community, including bacteria, fungi, and viruses. Microbiomes are found on all surfaces of our body and are essential to the normal functioning of our immune system and tissue homeostasis. Our work revealed that circumcision eliminates anaerobic bacteria from the penile microbiome. These anaerobic bacteria cause sub-clinical inflammation in penile skin, and this inflammation makes an individual more susceptible to HIV if they have unprotected sex with an infected partner. We are continuing to build upon this work, to (1) understand the virulence mechanism by which anaerobes are able to induce inflammation; (2) identify the specific inflammatory pathways involved, and (3) understand if and how genital microbiomes are shared between sex partners. Our long-term goal is to develop novel, non-surgical strategies to promote optimal genital microbiomes and health.

Current Projects

3D Foreskin Model

Our team is creating a 3D in vitro foreskin model through tissue engineering to model bacteria-human interactions. This artificial foreskin model will enable us to culture whole polymicrobial communities on full-depth skin, to explore how anaerobes induce inflammation and contribute to HIV susceptibility.

Adolescent Study

Approximately 40% of new HIV infections occur in adolescents, however little is known about how changes in the mucosa during puberty influence the penile microbiome, inflammation, and HIV risk. In collaboration with the Rakai Health Sciences Program (RHSP), we are following 200 adolescent males through the puberty (and for some, sexual debut) to determining how the penile microbiome and local immune milieu is altered by reproductive maturation and sexual debut, with the goal of understanding how this may impact HIV susceptibility.

Penile Microbiota and Antimicrobial Agents

Based on our observation that the penile microbiome can increase local inflammation and HIV susceptibility, the XXX was a randomized controlled trial designed to determine if oral and topical antibiotic treatments can reduce penile anaerobes and inflammation. Our lab is performing immunofluorescence studies to assess if treatments effect epithelial integrity and HIV target cell density in foreskin tissue.
